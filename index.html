Where the homestead land went
Michael Jr. & Margaret stayed on the original 1904 homestead, purchasing an additional 469 acres from 1950 on. The line then passes to daughter Linda Mikita (m. Ben Hendrix, 1977), who took over the operation — exiting the Mikita surname. Meanwhile, the Section 14 tract inherited through John & Sophia's estate, and the Section 36 tract sold by Frank & Susie, both flow into Ernest L. & Jean Ann Mikita's consolidation and eventually the Mikita Living Trust — detailed fully in the Land section below.

Where They Actually Touch

Bridges Between the Two Families

Every documented marriage, land, or parish connection between Family A and Family B — none of it blood.

01 · The Murin marriages John, Frank, and Michael Jr. (Family A) each married a Murin sister/half-sister from the same blended household north of Ramah.
02 · Shared parish & cemetery Both families anchor at St. Mary's Eastern Orthodox Church (built 1905) and St. Michael's Cemetery, Calhan.
03 · Shared in-law surname pool Trojanovich, Murin, Sproch, Olyejar, Gdovin, Timora, Manyik recur across both families' in-law networks.
04 · Darlene M. Mikita's marriage-in A granddaughter of the separate Matt Sr. Trojanovich line married into the Mikita surname, land in Sec. 2 T10S/T11S R62W.
05 · Pallbearer overlap "Andy Mikita" (Family B) at Mike Jr.'s (Family A) funeral; "Larry Mikita" at both Andro Sr.'s and Mary's funerals.
06 · Same township, same era Michael Sr.'s 1904 patent and Andro Sr.'s 1914 patent sit in the same six-mile township, T10S R61W — likely root of the community's "first cousins" label.
07 · The old-country lead Both branches' candidate villages cluster in the same Šariš-region district — Tarna, Hradisko, Olejnikov, and the unresolved "Keno." Untested.
08 · The name collisions Two unrelated "John Mikita"s and three "Andrew Mikita"s — the texture of one small parish running on a narrow surname and naming pool, not evidence of kinship.

Two roots, planted the same year, in the same township — married, buried, and remembered into each other for four generations, without ever sharing an ancestor.
